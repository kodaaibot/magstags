Google Image Thumbnails for Organic Results: Case Study

I checked 9,718 listings. Here's what Google actually shows.

Thumbnail presence in organic results is hard to measure at scale — so I ran my own test to understand it better: 293 non-branded women's fashion keywords, both devices, two US locations, 9,718 organic listings scanned via API. The short version: mobile gets nearly twice the visual real estate. The longer version is more interesting.

Companion piece: Google Image Thumbnails in Organic — The Technical Reference covers the how — what to implement so Google can actually pull your images. This article covers the what — which keywords, intents, and positions get thumbnails.

§
A note on scope

The numbers below are shaped by the keywords I chose. Different seeds, different patterns, different categories would produce different rates. This is a structured sample of one vertical, not a universal benchmark. Treat it as a directional snapshot — useful for spotting patterns and relative differences, not as ground truth for "what percentage of all Google results have thumbnails."

How I tested this

I built a keyword set using a seed × pattern system: 39 product seeds (leggings, midi dress, blazer, ankle boots — across 9 fashion categories) crossed with 8 search modifier patterns that people type. That's 312 possible combinations, but 19 were dropped — all from the "fit + seed" pattern (e.g. "high waisted blazer") where the modifier produces a query nobody actually searches. The remaining 293 keywords all reflect real search behaviour.

The patterns ranged from bare product terms ("leggings") through colour modifiers ("black leggings"), "best" queries ("best leggings"), year-qualified reviews ("best leggings 2026"), outfit styling ("leggings outfit ideas"), and how-to queries ("how to style leggings"). This gave me systematic coverage across four intent groups: product + modifier, product, comparison, and informational.

I hit each keyword against Google's live organic results via the DataForSEO API, running separate mobile and desktop crawls. I ran the full set twice: once geo-locked to the US nationally, then again locked to Los Angeles to check for geo variation.

Combined dataset: 9,718 organic results and 19,968 images catalogued across both locations. The US national numbers (4,823 results, 9,741 images) form the primary analysis. LA data (4,895 results, 10,227 images) is used for the geo comparison section.

Mobile leads

Semrush Sensor data (April 2026) puts image thumbnails at 49% of US desktop SERPs and 71% of mobile across all categories. Shopping is higher: 77% desktop, 88% mobile. So thumbnails aren't rare. But "thumbnails appear a lot" doesn't tell you which keywords get them, which intents trigger them, or how many images Google attaches per listing. That's where it gets more granular.

Across all nine categories and all four intent types in this dataset, mobile serves more thumbnails on average. At the individual pattern level there's one exception — "fit + seed" queries where desktop edges ahead — but the overall direction is consistent.

Mobile gets almost twice the visual real estate

But the thumbnail rate only tells half the story. Among organic results that have thumbnails, count the total images. Across the same 293 keywords, mobile served 6,409 total images where desktop served 3,332. That's 92% more images on mobile than desktop.

What this means visually: mobile displays image strips — multiple product photos in a horizontal scroll — for each listing. Desktop shows fewer images per listing, or a single image. When a mobile listing gets a thumbnail, it gets more images. Desktop shows fewer.

Search intent changes everything

Aggregating thumbnail rates by query type reveals where the device gap widens. The pattern is consistent: queries that signal stronger commercial intent get more thumbnails on both devices, and queries that signal informational intent collapse on desktop while staying high on mobile.

Does location matter?

Running the same 293-keyword set geo-locked to Los Angeles instead of US national produced near-identical thumbnail rates. The headline numbers moved by less than 2 percentage points in either direction. Geo-personalisation for fashion appears to be minimal at this granularity.

Caveat for verticals where location matters

Fashion is a low-geo-variance vertical. For locally-anchored queries (restaurants, dentists, plumbers), geo will move thumbnail rates substantially. Don't generalise from this dataset to verticals where location is the dominant ranking signal.

What actually triggers a thumbnail?

The SERP data tells me where thumbnails appear. It doesn't tell me why — what's actually on the source page that makes Google decide to pull an image. That requires a different methodology: crawling the destination URLs and analysing their image implementation. That's Part 3.

What I can say from this dataset: the variance between domains with similar appearance counts is large enough that something on the page is driving the difference. Nordstrom at 98% mobile and Amazon at 73% — same keywords, same intent — points to differences in image markup, OG tags, schema, image count on page, or some combination. The data tells me who; it doesn't yet tell me what.

What to do with this

If you sell physical products in any visual category — fashion, home, beauty, electronics, anything where a photo of the thing helps — the headline takeaways:

  • Mobile is where you're competing for visual real estate. Desktop strategies that ignore image implementation are leaving 92% more available image impressions on the table.
  • Positions 1 and 2 get a thumbnail premium. Below that, it's roughly equal opportunity. If you're in the top three, the marginal click value of an image is higher than at position 6.
  • Informational queries collapse on desktop. "Outfit ideas", "how to style", year-qualified reviews — Google decides those readers want text. Plan content for the device that surfaces it.
  • Audit the domains that beat you. If Nordstrom is at 98% and you're at 60% on the same keyword, the gap is on your end. Look at their image markup before you look at their content.

Run this for your vertical

The methodology generalises. If you want to run a comparable analysis for your category, the seed × pattern × intent framework is the part to copy. The pattern groups will be different (B2B keywords behave nothing like fashion), but the structure of "controlled crosses, both devices, two geos, image counts not just thumbnail rates" applies broadly. Part 3 will publish the crawl methodology for the destination pages — different work, different tool, same dataset feeding it.
